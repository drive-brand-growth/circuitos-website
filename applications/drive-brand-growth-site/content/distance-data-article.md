# Distance Data: What Your Customers' Drive Time Tells You That Their Transaction Never Will

*By Noel Pena*

---

Every car wash operator in America can tell you how many cars they washed yesterday. Ask them how far those cars drove to get there, and the room goes silent.

That silence is where the money lives.

I've spent 15 years in retail data — first building LPR marketing strategies at DRN, a Motorola Solutions company, then licensing MetroFlex Gym from a single Arlington, Texas location to an internationally recognized brand operating across the United States and Japan. In both industries, I kept hitting the same wall: operators measured what happened at the point of sale but were completely blind to the journey that brought the customer there.

So I named it. I called it **Distance Data**.

## What Distance Data Actually Is

Distance Data is the marketing intelligence derived from understanding how far a customer travels to reach your location — and what that distance reveals about their intent, their loyalty risk, and their lifetime value.

It's not a metric on your POS screen. Your register doesn't know if a customer drove 2 miles or 22 miles. But your license plate recognition system does. Your CRM can. Your data, if you're paying attention, already contains this signal. Most operators have never looked.

Here's what 15 years of data has taught me:

**A customer who drives past three competitors to reach you is not the same customer as someone who lives across the street.** The drive-past customer chose you. That choice is a signal. The convenience customer defaulted to you. That default is fragile.

Distance Data quantifies the difference.

## The Three Insights Distance Data Reveals

### 1. Intent Density

When I was building LPR marketing programs at DRN, we analyzed passerby traffic patterns for car wash operators across the country. We didn't just count cars — we measured where they came from, how frequently they passed, and at what distance they started converting from passerby to customer.

What we found surprised everyone: customers who traveled more than 7 miles to a car wash had a 34% higher retention rate than customers within a 2-mile radius. The industry assumed proximity was the primary driver of loyalty. The data said otherwise.

Proximity drives the first visit. Distance drives the relationship.

### 2. Market Radius Reality

Every franchise model and every licensing agreement includes a territory radius. At MetroFlex Gym, where I've managed the licensing program for 15 years, we use a 10-mile protected territory. But territory lines drawn on a map and actual customer behavior are different things.

Distance Data shows you where your real market boundary is — not the one on paper, but the one your customers draw with their driving patterns. Some locations pull from 15 miles. Others struggle to draw from 3. The difference isn't location quality. It's brand gravity.

When we evaluated the MetroFlex international expansion into Japan — two locations in Soka City and Chiba — Distance Data principles informed site selection. We didn't just ask "is there demand?" We asked "how far will customers in this market travel for a hardcore training experience?" In Tokyo's dense metro area, a customer traveling 30 minutes by train is the equivalent of a Texas customer driving 20 miles. The distance changes. The intent signal doesn't.

### 3. Competitive Vulnerability

If your customers drive 10 miles to reach you and a competitor opens at mile 5, you have a problem you can see coming before it arrives. Distance Data gives you early warning.

More importantly, it shows you which customers are vulnerable and which aren't. The customer who drives 12 miles past a closer competitor to reach you is your most loyal customer. The customer who drives 2 miles because you're the closest option is your most vulnerable.

Most operators don't know the difference until the competitor opens and it's too late.

## Why This Matters Now

We're in an era where every retail operator has access to more data than they can process. LPR technology, mobile location data, CRM systems, loyalty programs — the signals are everywhere. But most operators are drowning in transaction data and starving for decision data.

Distance Data is decision data. It tells you where to spend your marketing budget (target the 5-8 mile ring where intent is high but awareness is low). It tells you which customers to fight for (the drive-past loyalists, not the convenience defaulters). It tells you where your next location should be (where the distance-to-intent ratio is highest).

I introduced this concept working with car wash operators at DRN, but the principle is universal. I've applied it to gym licensing at MetroFlex, to event marketing, to retail site selection. Any business with a physical location and customers who choose to travel to it has Distance Data waiting to be read.

The question is whether you're reading it — or just counting transactions.

## The Connection to Transactional Blindness

Distance Data is the cure for what I call Transactional Blindness — the condition where retailers see the sale but miss the customer. If you're only measuring what happens at the register, you're blind to the most valuable signal in your business: the customer who chose to drive to you.

That choice is the data point that predicts everything else — retention, lifetime value, referral potential, churn risk. And it's invisible to every POS system on the market.

Distance Data makes it visible.
